For this project, we wanted to explore the limitations of human rights for specific groups in the United States and compare the limitations to the same groups in other regions across the globe. To achieve this, we used the ‘people_at_risk’ dataset, which contains the results from 2018 to 2023 of the annual Human Rights Measurement Initiative Expert Survey. This survey asks human rights practitioners in each country which groups they believed were at risk of having certain human rights restricted or violated. Through this dataset, we could access the total number of respondents for each country, the number of respondents who considered a given right to be at risk, and the proportion of respondents who deemed a given right to be at risk. Our groups of interest for the three research questions are Children, older people, and people with disabilities.

Our first research question uses a multi-line time series to compare the yearly change in the restriction/violation of the human right to health in the United States for each interest group. The second research question considers every right available in the dataset via a heatmap. Instead of looking at the proportion by year, the heatmap displays the average proportion for the recorded years in the United States. Finally, our third research question uses a bar graph to examine the average proportion of risk to the right to freedom from torture in each continental region.

    'Children':     12,

    'Disabilities': 14

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 4))
for label, code in groups.items():
    col = f'{right}_atrisk_prop{code}'
    ts  = us[['year', col]].dropna().sort_values('year')
    plt.plot(ts['year'], ts[col], marker='o', label=label)

plt.title(f"At-Risk of {right.capitalize()} Over Time in the US by Group")
plt.xlabel('Year')
plt.ylabel('At-Risk Proportion')
plt.legend()
plt.xticks(ts['year'], rotation=45)
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

This graph is a multi-line time series that displays the proportion of human rights practitioners in the United States who believed a group was at risk of limitation or violation of their right to health for each year between 2018 and 2023. Based on the graph, people with disabilities were thought to be the most at risk each year among the three groups (Children, older people, and people with disabilities), while children were consistently thought to have the lowest risk. Children and older people experienced virtually the same magnitude of increase and decrease in at-risk proportion each year, except for between 2021 and 2022, where children experienced a high enough increase to be level with older people. Children also experienced a bigger decrease than older people between 2022 and 2023. People with disabilities also experienced increases and decreases corresponding with the other groups, but the magnitudes of change differed more significantly. An interesting trend is that between 2020 and 2021, the proportion increased slightly for people with disabilities but decreased notably for the other two groups. This could be because stricter regulations from the COVID-19 pandemic decreased risk for children and older people while the presence of the pandemic limited those prone to disease. The increase between 2021 and 2022 could be due to increased immunity and looser regulation of the pandemic.

This chart is a color‑coded grid showing how often, on average, experts around the world flagged each of fourteen civil and political rights as a risk for children, older adults, or people with disabilities. Each row is a different right (for example, “right to protest,” “right to free speech,” “freedom from torture,” “right to education,” and so on), and each column is one of our three groups. The darker the square, the higher the share of experts saying that group is vulnerable for that right. For instance, you can immediately see that people with disabilities face the highest average risk (darkest color) when it comes to health‑related rights, while children and older adults are relatively less at risk in that area. Scanning across a given row shows which group experts worry about most for that specific right; scanning down a column highlights which rights pose the greatest overall threat to that group.

    'Colombia': 'Americas', 'Peru': 'Americas', 'Venezuela': 'Americas',
    # Oceania
    'Australia': 'Oceania', 'New Zealand': 'Oceania',
    'Papua New Guinea': 'Oceania', 'Fiji': 'Oceania',
}
df['region'] = df['country'].map(region_map)

# define the three groups
groups    = {'Children':12, 'Older':13, 'Disabilities':14}
tort_cols = {lab: f'tort_atrisk_prop{code}' for lab,code in groups.items()}

# pick the year you want:
selected_year = 2019   # ← change this to any year in your data

# filter
df_year = df[df['year'] == selected_year]

# compute regional averages (filling in zeros if a column is missing)
regional_avgs = (
    df_year
      .groupby('region')
      .agg({c:'mean' for c in tort_cols.values() if c in df_year})
      .reset_index()
)
for c in tort_cols.values():
    if c not in regional_avgs:
        regional_avgs[c] = 0
regional_avgs = regional_avgs[['region'] + list(tort_cols.values())]

# plot
x     = np.arange(len(regional_avgs))
width = 0.25

plt.figure(figsize=(10,6))
for i,(label,col) in enumerate(tort_cols.items()):
    plt.bar(x + i*width,
            regional_avgs[col],
            width,
            label=label)

plt.xticks(x + width, regional_avgs['region'], rotation=45, ha='right')
plt.ylabel('Avg Torture At-Risk Proportion')
plt.title(f'Regional Avg “Torture” Risk by Group ({selected_year})')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

This clustered bar chart compares the latest-year share of experts in five world regions who named children, older adults, or people with disabilities as at risk of torture. Each cluster of three bars represents a region—Africa, Asia, Europe, the Americas, and Oceania—with blue for children, orange for older people, and green for persons with disabilities. The height of each bar is the percentage of regional experts identifying that group’s risk. In the Americas, the blue bar (children) towers above the others, showing experts there see children as especially vulnerable. In Europe, the orange bar (older adults) is highest, indicating seniors face the greatest concern. Other regions display more balanced heights, signaling that in those areas the three groups are viewed as similarly at risk.
